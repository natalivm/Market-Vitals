01 · Foundation

What institutional money flow actually is

A retail trader buys 50 shares — it vanishes into the book and moves nothing. An institution works positions worth hundreds of millions: one market order would run price away and wreck its entry. So it slices the position into thousands of small orders over weeks — algos, icebergs, dark pools. Hiding completely is impossible; volume and structure give it away. Our job is to read the trail.

TRAIL №1

Volume without price progress

Huge volume trades while price sits in a tight range — someone is absorbing everything being sold. That's accumulation.

TRAIL №2

Aggressive move off a level

Price explodes out of a range in wide candles — accumulation is complete, the institution lets price run. That's an impulse.

TRAIL №3

A defended level

Price returns to where the move started — and gets bought again. Unfilled orders are still sitting there. That's a demand zone.

Key idea

We don't guess what funds "think." We read the mechanical consequence of their work: large capital physically cannot enter and exit without leaving marks on price and volume.

02 · Candle language

Impulse vs Balance — two phases that always alternate

The market breathes in two modes. Impulse is the movement phase: one side fully dominates. Balance is equilibrium: the big player is resting, or quietly building / unloading a position. Every trend is a chain of "impulse → balance → impulse" — exactly what's annotated on your NVDA weekly markup.

Entry rule

Enter from balance, in the direction of the previous impulse — or on a confirmed break out of balance. Buying in the middle of an impulse that's already 3–4 candles old means providing exit liquidity to whoever accumulated in the balance.

Structure first — color is secondary

Read structure before color. Where price sits — the swing highs/lows (HH/HL/LL/LH), impulse vs balance, distance from a zone — is the story and the odds. A single green or red candle is one bar's outcome; on its own it's noise. A red candle inside an uptrend on demand is usually a buy, not a sell. And even when color counts, the close location (near the high or the low) says more than the color: a green candle closing on its low is weak.

WHEN COLOR MATTERS · 1

At a mapped level

The reaction candle at your zone is the tell: a wide green impulse off demand, or a wide red rejection off supply. That's the market showing its hand where you were already watching.

WHEN COLOR MATTERS · 2

On the trigger candle

The single bar that earns the entry — the reclaim, the break, the confirmation. Body size, close near the extreme, and color all count here, backed by above-average volume.

WHEN COLOR MATTERS · 3

To grade an impulse

A run of same-color bodies on rising volume names the winning side. But away from a level, in balance, alternating colors are expected — don't trade them.

The hierarchy

Structure sets the plan → the level sets the location → color only confirms the trigger. If you ever find yourself trading because "the candle turned green," with no structure and no level behind it, you've inverted the whole method.

03 · The level map

Demand and supply zones: where unfilled orders live

When an institution accumulates in balance and price impulses away, part of its orders stay unfilled. That base — the impulse origin — becomes a demand zone; the mirror (an impulse-down origin, or the long upper wicks of distribution) is a supply zone. When price returns, the leftover orders fire.

Demand zone · how to draw it

Find the last balance before the impulse up. Zone = from the balance low to its upper boundary. On NVDA that's 170–177 — the base the trend launched from, retested in March–April and held. Your monthly markup drew it as a green box.

Supply zone · how to draw it

The distribution range under the highs: long upper wicks, heavy red candles, price failing to hold above. On NVDA that's 207.98–231.83 — the red box on your monthly markup. Sellers hit 207.98 repeatedly.

The retested top and the retested bottom — how we define them

A level is not the first touch — it's the retest. The first time price reacts at a high or low, you only have a hypothesis that big orders are there. You get proof when price returns and does the same thing again. That return is the retest, and it's what turns a random swing point into a tradable level.

Defining the retested top

Find the origin of an impulse down, a cluster of long upper wicks (distribution), or two highs at the same price (a double top). Mark it. It's confirmed the moment price returns and is rejected again. On NVDA: 207.98 was hit and rejected two days running; the 212.5 double top is a retest by definition; 231.83 is the outer distribution top under the ATH.

Defining the retested bottom

Find the last balance/base before the impulse up, or a swing low with long lower wicks (absorption). Mark from the balance low to its upper edge. It's confirmed the moment price returns and holds again. On NVDA: 170–177 is the impulse origin, retested in March–April and held (the green box); 197.84 is the near-term balance floor being tested now.

How do we know the orders weren't filled? The candles tell you

A bank that finished filling its order leaves nothing behind — price would pass straight through. We know orders remain when price returns and reacts: it gets pushed away. That push has a candle signature.

  • Rejection candle (pin bar). A long wick spears into the zone and the body closes back out — price was physically pushed away. The #1 tell that orders remain.
  • Impulse candle off the level. A wide-body candle leaving the zone on above-average volume: the big player is active and defending its average price.
  • Absorption candle. Heavy volume but a small range right at the level — one side is quietly soaking up the other. More orders are still parked there.

Why a zone "works" — and when it stops working

A zone isn't a magic line — it's a probability that large orders are still present. The first test is strongest: the most orders are there. Every retest eats some of that liquidity. If price returns a third or fourth time in quick succession, or breaks through with an impulse candle on volume — the orders are exhausted, the zone breaks, and it often flips (demand becomes supply, and vice versa).

Classic mistake

Buying "because price came down to a level." A level without reaction is just a price from the past. We need confirmation: absorption of selling + an impulse candle off the zone. Without that, the level is a hypothesis, not a signal.

So how do we catch the move — if we can't be sure?

You just named the whole game. In balance, buy and sell orders are matched and price rotates. The instant one side is left with unfilled orders — more buyers than sellers — price impulses that way. But nobody knows in advance which way balance breaks. So we don't try to be right. We enter where being wrong is cheap: at the edge of the zone, stop just beyond it, target the far side. Small risk, big reward.

  • Location creates the edge, not prediction. At a level your stop is close, so risk is small; the next zone is far, so reward is large. Mid-range, both are equal — no edge.
  • Wait for the trigger to raise the odds. A rejection/impulse candle off the zone is the market showing its hand — it lifts your win-rate on top of the good R/R.
  • Asymmetry survives being wrong. At 1:3, you can lose 6 of 10 and still profit. You don't need to be right often — you need to be right cheaply.
The point

Being right isn't the goal — positive expectancy is. Tight R/R at a level is what lets a trader who's wrong half the time still make money. (Play with the exact math in the autopsy's EV calculator.)

04 · Structure

Uptrend, downtrend, and the moment the uptrend "isn't there anymore"

An uptrend is a sequence of higher highs (HH) and higher lows (HL). A downtrend mirrors it: lower lows (LL) and lower highs (LH). Trend is defined by swing structure, not by an indicator. A trend break needs both: price fails a new HH and takes out the last HL.

Read the trend top-down

NVDA monthly and weekly are still in an uptrend (HH/HL intact, price above the 170–177 demand). The daily uptrend is broken. That's not a contradiction — it's context: a correction inside a larger trend.

What that means for a long

The higher timeframe says "buying pullbacks is allowed." The lower one says "not right now." A timeframe conflict = wait until the lower timeframe confirms a reversal at a higher-timeframe level.

09 · Reading vs drawing

Where the retail "support line" case breaks

Here's how most retail traders reason about this exact chart: "draw a line at 190 — it's support, so it bounces; and as long as we hold the range, we break out to new highs." It feels rigorous. But it inverts our whole method: it reads drawn lines instead of liquidity, and builds a story to fit a bias that's already bullish. The volume profile on the right side of the chart quietly refutes it.

What the chart actually shows

  • Structure: the daily already put in a lower high under the 207.98–231.83 supply and is chopping mid-range — the uptrend is broken, not "intact." Ascending trendlines are drawn over that break.
  • Volume profile (VPVR): the fattest node — the point of control — sits around 180–185, not 190. Volume through ~190–202 is thin. Price falls fast through thin nodes; it rests at fat ones.
  • The tool's own signals: sell-triangles fired near 209 and 230 (distribution into supply); buy-triangles fired near 170 and 178 — i.e. the demand that actually worked is below, not at 190.
  • Momentum & RSI: mid-range, no oversold-at-a-level reversal. Nothing is confirming a bounce — the case is a prediction, not a reaction.

The one root error

It's all a single mistake wearing five hats: a story built to justify a bias. Retail draws the line first and finds reasons it should hold; flow finds where the orders are (the profile, the retested zones) and waits for price to react there. Lines are free and infinite — you can draw one that says anything. Liquidity is fixed: it sits at the fat part of the profile and at zones that already produced a reaction. Trade the liquidity, not the line.

The caveat — a wall is not a zone

A demand/supply zone is resting institutional orders: structural, and it persists. A dealer gamma wall / pin is a hedging artifact that expires (Aug 21 OPEX) and can flip. Use walls for timing and confluence, zones for the thesis. When gamma flips negative — price loses $200, or after OPEX — the pin breaks and moves amplify. That's exactly when our reclaim/breakdown triggers finally get their impulse.

Verdict

Yes — it supports the theory, as confluence + regime, not a new thesis. Same ceiling (~207.98–210), same defended support (200 tactically, 170–177 structurally), same conclusion: range-bound, wait for the break.
