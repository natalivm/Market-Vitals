The read · money flow

The whole institutional cycle, on one chart

NBIS didn’t just go up and come down — it walked through every stage of the flow model. Reading those stages is what tells you where you are before you place a trade. Right now the flush left a broken daily structure and a weak bounce grinding back up into the resistance it lost. That’s the setup: sell the bounce where it fails, ride the correction down.

Stage 1 · Accumulation

The 49 base

The long, quiet shelf near 49.39 is where size was built — unfilled buy orders stacking with no urgency. Boring by design. That base is the launchpad for everything above it.

Stage 2 · Markup

The parabola to 353

Impulse after impulse — institutions marking price up through 100 → 199.90 → 222 → 281 to the 353.14 all-time high. Every dip got bought; the candles are big-bodied and one-directional. This is trend, not chop.

Stage 3 · Distribution

The 281–300 top

Weekly RSI printed ≈86 — climactic. Big upper wicks and heavy red candles at the highs = smart money selling into euphoria while retail chased. Supply overwhelmed demand and the trend rolled.

Stage 4 · Flush → balance

The July flush, then the coil

Price broke 222 and 199.90 and ran stops down to a ~163 low (7/17). It has since bounced, but weakly — 7/20 closed 182.62 (after-hours ~187.5), still under the wall. Daily RSI 41.24 and MACD −13.80 (bearish, expanding): the correction has room to run once the bounce fails.

Where we are right now

182.62 (after-hours ~187.5), a weak bounce grinding up toward the 190.49 wall — still below the 199.90 broken support, the 194.28 nine-EMA and the 205 two-hundred-hour EMA. This is a counter-trend bounce inside a broken daily structure. The edge is selling that bounce where it fails (190.49), for the move back down to the 200-day (149.44). Chasing it lower here, mid-air, is not the trade.

Takeaway Sell the failure at 190.49 — tight stop 192 for the R, or ATR stop 202–213 (~$23 ATR) for room — and ride it to the 200-day at 149.44. Precise entry keeps the risk small; ATR tells you what “room” actually costs.

The one paid gap — and the free workaround

The only row you can’t build for free is Dealer Mechanics / GEX (row 06). Services like SpotGamma or Menthor Q sell it, but you can approximate it yourself: pull NBIS’s full options chain (free, delayed — CBOE or yfinance), sum open-interest × gamma × 100 × spot across strikes, and flip the sign for puts — that gives a usable gamma-exposure proxy and the zero-gamma flip level. Everything else on this list is either already on your charts or one free download away.

Today’s trade · one full trade

One full trade to the end — short, tight stop

One direction, held to target. The dominant flow is down — the parabola distributed, the daily structure is broken (price under 199.90 and the 205 two-hundred-hour EMA), and this bounce is a weak balance beneath resistance. The full move is the air pocket to the 200-day EMA (149.44), where the correction naturally ends. And the entry is one hard line — short 190.49, the level the range keeps failing at. That gives you a choice of stop: a tight one just above the line (192, best R), or an ATR-sized one for room (202 at 0.5× ATR, up to 213 at a full 1× ATR of ~$23). Precise entry = small risk; trend alignment = full target.

Why this stop is tight (and your last one wasn’t)

Your earlier short entered a zone (185–200) with the stop out at 213 — $13–28 of risk, because the invalidation was the far side of a wide area. This one does the opposite: it enters at the single line (190.49) and stops just past it (192) — ~$1.5 risk for the same ~43-point target. The target didn’t shrink; the risk did. That is the entire trick to a tight stop: let the level define it, not your account size or your hope.

The real ATR reframes the wide stop

NBIS’s daily ATR(14) is 22.73 (Yahoo) / 24.02 (TradingView) — ~$23, far higher than a $13–16 guess. That one number reshapes the whole stop question. The tight 192 is barely 0.065× ATR — a sliver of a normal day’s range, so it will get wicked unless the rejection at 190.49 is clean and instant. It is a scalp-entry stop (re-enter if hit), not a swing stop. A stop that truly gives room has to be measured in ATRs: 0.5× ≈ 202, 1× ≈ 213. And notice — a full 1-ATR stop lands at ~213, which is exactly where you first put it. That instinct was right; the fix was never “tighter for its own sake,” it was pairing the ATR stop with a precise line (190.49) instead of a wide zone.

The catch on a $23-ATR name (and how to run both)

High ATR forces a real tradeoff. The tight 192 gives huge R (27R+ to target) but low hit-rate — you’ll get shaken out often and re-enter. The 1×-ATR 213 almost never gets noise-hit, but the same target is only ~1.9R — staying power, poor payout. The 0.5× 202 is the middle (~3.6R, sits through most chop). Best of both: run two — a starter on 192, the core on 202. A wick to 192 costs only the starter; the core rides. Once 175.58 breaks, trail to breakeven and the stop question is closed.

How “to the end” actually works

You don’t sit through 27R with the opening stop — you trail. The instant 175.58 breaks, move the stop to your entry (190.5): now it’s a risk-free trade running to the 200-EMA. Scale a third off at 164 and 154, ride the last third to ~149 with the stop trailing each prior level. That is how one entry becomes a full-move trade: small fixed risk at the start, then house money the rest of the way. Below 149 is a different trade (trend change → 141 / 118) — don’t improvise it, re-plan it.
